# PSTH (peri-stimulus time histogram)

## Origin in electrophysiology

PSTH is a term inherited from electrophysiology. In the spike-counting world, you take a long extracellular recording, find every spike, line up a window around each behavioural event of interest (cue, reward, lever press), and count spikes in fine time bins relative to that event. Average across events and you get a histogram that says: "in the 200 ms after the cue, the neuron fires at roughly 30 Hz on average; before the cue, it fires at about 10 Hz." The histogram shape is the neuron's average response, time-locked to the event.

In fiber photometry the operation is identical in spirit but the underlying signal is different. Instead of discrete spikes you have a continuous fluorescence trace (typically z-scored dF/F) that proxies bulk calcium activity in the recorded region. There is nothing to count, only to slice. The "histogram" becomes a continuous amplitude trace: an average of the z-score waveform locked to the event.

The "histogram" name is therefore a vestige of the electrophysiology origin. In photometry it is more accurate to call it an *event-aligned average*, but the field has standardised on PSTH and that is what GuPPy uses.

## Constructing and reading a PSTH

A PSTH is the across-event average of a signal in a fixed window around each event. At each time point relative to the event, the curve value is the mean signal over the events in the list, and the shading band is the standard error of that mean. The inputs that define a PSTH are a continuous z-scored signal, a list of event timestamps, and pre and post window lengths in seconds. Baseline correction, binning, and summary statistics are layers on top of this skeleton.

PSTHs are computed on the z-scored trace rather than on raw fluorescence or dF/F because z-score puts different recordings on a comparable scale, with values expressed in standard-deviations-of-noise units. A comparable scale is a necessary condition for cross-session pooling but not a sufficient one: whether a pooled result is interpretable also depends on biology, preprocessing, and experimental design. The full breakdown of which z-score variant best suits PSTH, and the cases where computing on dF/F directly is preferable, is in [`zscore.md`](zscore.md).

![Four-row figure walking through the PSTH operation. Row 1: full session z-score trace with event timestamps as green vertical lines. Row 2: same trace with each pre/post extraction window shaded blue. Row 3: every extracted window overlaid on a shared event-relative time axis, showing both the consistent event-locked peak and the event-by-event variability around it. Row 4: the event-averaged PSTH with SEM band, isolating the event-locked component.](../_static/images/psth_explainer/fig1_psth_walkthrough.svg)

Single event-aligned traces are dominated by noise and by signal unrelated to the event, as the row-3 overlay shows: alongside the typical responses sit a pure-noise trace (orange) with no event-locked activity and an artifact trace (purple) with a spurious post-event peak. The event-aligned average is what separates the event-locked component from everything else. Non-event-locked activity (spontaneous transients, drift, the purple trace's artifact) lands at random offsets across events and smears out. What row 4 isolates is therefore the event-locked response by definition: the part of the signal that consistently appears at the same time across events. With small event counts the loudest individual traces can dominate the average and the SEM (which shrinks as `1/sqrt(N)`) becomes correspondingly unreliable; how many counts as "too few" depends on the recording's SNR and the across-event variability, but the underlying limit is a study-design constraint that the analysis cannot rescue.

The flip side is that PSTH cannot reveal activity that is not consistently locked to the chosen event; transient detection and [cross-correlation](cross_correlation.md) are the GuPPy tools for those questions.

### Correction for long-term drift

Long photometry sessions are not flat. The trace drifts slowly up and down across the recording for reasons unrelated to the events of interest, including residual photobleaching, gradual changes in the animal's general state, and slow shifts at the rig. Z-scoring the whole session does not remove this drift. So an event-aligned trace extracted from early in the session has a different starting height than one extracted from late in the session, and the average of those traces inherits the spread: the mean PSTH no longer sits cleanly at zero before the event, and the SEM band is widened by event-to-event differences in starting height that have nothing to do with the response.

![Three-panel figure. Top: a 600-second z-score trace with a strong monotonic drift carrying the baseline from roughly z = +1.5 at the start of the session down to roughly z = -1.5 at the end; ten event windows are spread across the session, three of them highlighted in blue, red, and green at the start, middle, and end while the other seven are shaded pale gray. Middle: the three highlighted event-aligned traces extracted on a shared event-relative time axis; each has the same event-locked peak and undershoot, but their pre-event baselines sit at clearly different levels (positive z for the start-of-session trace, near zero for the middle, negative z for the end), reflecting where in the session each event happened to occur. Bottom: the same three event-aligned traces after per-event pre-event mean subtraction; every trace now starts at zero pre-event and the response itself is unchanged.](../_static/images/psth_explainer/fig2_session_drift.svg)

To understand why z-score does not remove drift, notice that the mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$ in $z = (x - \mu) / \sigma$ are *single numbers* computed over the entire recording, not functions of time. Subtracting $\mu$ from every sample shifts the whole trace down by the same amount, so it removes only the constant part of any drift, not its time-varying part. Concretely: if the drift is linear, $\text{drift}(t) = a \cdot t + b$, then $\mu = a \cdot T/2 + b$, and subtracting $\mu$ leaves $a \cdot t - a \cdot T/2$, still linear in $t$ with the slope $a$ unchanged. More generally, z-score is an affine map applied uniformly in time: it can shift and scale any input but not change its shape. Whatever time-varying structure was in $x(t)$ is also in $z(t)$. Removing drift therefore requires a *time-aware* operation, one that uses a different baseline at each time point.

GuPPy's per-event baseline correction is the simplest version of such a time-aware fix: rather than modelling drift across the whole session, it just removes whatever the drift contributed to each event-aligned trace's pre-event window. The mean of each trace's pre-event sub-window is subtracted from that trace, anchoring every trace at zero pre-event regardless of where in the session the event happened (visible in the bottom panel of the figure). This sits on top of the session-wide z-score rather than replacing it: the two together give you a y-axis in noise units (from z-score) that starts cleanly at zero pre-event (from baseline correction). The subtraction window has to be a genuine baseline: if it extends past t = 0 it includes part of the post-event response and artificially flattens the PSTH.

### Summary statistics: peak and AUC

Once a PSTH has been built and corrected for drift, two scalars summarise its response into a number that can be compared across conditions, sessions, or animals. *Peak amplitude* is the largest value of the PSTH inside a chosen post-event window. *AUC* (area under the curve) is the integral over the same window. The figure below applies both to three different response shapes.

![3x2 grid of PSTH plots. Each row shows the same response shape twice: peak amplitude on the left panel (red marker at the maximum, dotted vertical line dropping to zero, peak value labelled in the corner) and AUC on the right panel (green fill under the curve across the post-event window, AUC value labelled in the corner). Top row: a clean canonical Gaussian peak; peak and AUC give consistent moderate-magnitude readings. Middle row: a low-amplitude broad sustained response; peak is small but AUC is large because the trace stays elevated for several seconds. Bottom row: a small real response with a tall narrow artifact spike; peak latches onto the artifact and reports a large value while AUC averages across the window and stays close to the real response.](../_static/images/psth_explainer/fig3_peak_vs_auc.svg)

Peak is intuitive and maps directly onto how papers report response magnitude. In the canonical case (top row), where the response is a single clean event-locked peak, that intuition serves you well: peak and AUC tell the same story about a moderate-sized response. The lower two rows are where the metrics start to disagree. A broad sustained response (middle row) has a small peak even though the trace stays elevated for several seconds, and AUC captures the magnitude that peak misses because integration adds across time. The opposite case (bottom row) is a modest real response contaminated by a brief artifact spike: peak latches onto the artifact and reports a value much larger than the real response, while AUC averages across the window and dilutes the artifact's contribution to something closer to the real signal.

The two metrics are therefore complementary. Reporting both is common practice because together they distinguish "taller in this condition" from "taller and broader in this condition", and they protect against the failure mode where a single noisy sample dominates the summary.

## Event rejection

Not every event timestamp produces a usable event-aligned trace. Some sit too close to the start or end of the recording for the extraction window to fit; some arrive in clusters tight enough that adjacent windows overlap and contaminate each other. Both cases would distort the average if left in. Two filters address these failure modes.

**Edge rejection** drops events whose pre or post extraction window would extend past the recording bounds. There is no data outside the recording, so the missing samples cannot be averaged honestly, and keeping such an event would distort the event-aligned average and any downstream statistics. The figure below shows this visually: the extraction window of an event near the recording start clips past t = 0, into a "no recording" zone where no data exists.

**Burst rejection** drops events that fall closer to a previous kept event than a user-set inter-event threshold. This matters for behaviours that come in bursts (rapid licks, repeated lever presses) where adjacent extraction windows would otherwise overlap and contaminate each other. The threshold is task-dependent and is a modelling choice rather than a tunable with a formal optimum: the same recording with two different thresholds produces two valid PSTHs that mean slightly different things.
